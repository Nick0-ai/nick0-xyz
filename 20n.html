20n Research Laboratory

The universal brain for robots.

Founded January 2026 · present
Founder Nick0 (Nicolas Philippe)
Location Paris, France
Stage Research / Prototype

20n is a biomimetic AI research laboratory building a single artificial brain that can control any robot body, with zero training, no cloud dependency, and a power consumption of just 20 watts on a laptop CPU. The architecture is grounded in Karl Friston's Active Inference and Liquid Neural Networks developed at MIT.

01 — The problem

The robotics industry has spent over $20 billion trying to build general purpose robots. The result, after decades of work and some of the most well funded labs on the planet, is that not a single general purpose robot ships at scale. Every robot today uses one model per task. Each skill requires millions of GPU hours to train. The resulting controllers are brittle the moment they encounter anything outside their training distribution. And the hardware draws hundreds of watts just to run inference.

This is not a compute problem. Throwing more GPUs at it will not fix it. Scaling the data will not fix it either. The problem is architectural. The entire industry is building one narrow specialist at a time, then trying to stitch them together into something that looks general. That approach has a ceiling, and the industry has already hit it.

Nobody has built a brain that works across different robot bodies. A brain you can drop into a bipedal walker, a quadruped, or a 17 joint humanoid, and have it figure things out on its own. The reason nobody has done it is because the dominant paradigm, deep reinforcement learning, was never designed to do it. It was designed to maximize a reward signal inside a fixed environment. That is a fundamentally different goal than building a system that can understand and adapt to any body in any situation.

02 — The solution

20n builds a universal artificial brain based on two foundational ideas from theoretical neuroscience and machine learning: Active Inference, the framework proposed by Karl Friston in 2010 that describes how biological brains work, and Liquid Neural Networks, the family of continuous time neural networks developed at MIT by Radu Hasani, Mathias Lechner, and their collaborators.

The core claim is simple. One brain. One set of parameters. Any robot body. You do not retrain it. You do not fine tune it. You drop it in, and it figures out how to move. It learns the physics of its own body in real time, builds a world model on the fly, and selects actions by predicting the future and choosing the path that minimizes uncertainty.

03 — Architecture

The architecture is hierarchical and biomimetic, modeled directly after the structure of the biological brain. Five layers operate at different frequencies, each responsible for a distinct level of abstraction. They communicate vertically through prediction errors, just like the neocortical hierarchy in mammals.

Spinal Cord 1,000 Hz
Hardwired PD (proportional derivative) reflexes and posture control. This is the fastest loop in the system. It reacts before the brain "thinks." When the body is falling, the spinal cord corrects joint positions within a single millisecond. No learning, no inference, just physics. This layer keeps the robot upright while higher layers plan.
Cerebellum 100 Hz
Internal body model built through forward simulation. Uses CfC (Closed form Continuous depth) Liquid Neural Networks. The cerebellum predicts where every joint will be 10 milliseconds into the future. If the prediction does not match reality, it corrects instantly. This is how the brain learns the physics of a new body in seconds, not hours.
Cortex 10 Hz
The world model. Learns online by minimizing variational free energy, which is a mathematical proxy for prediction error. Uses LTC (Liquid Time Constant) networks. The cortex does not require a training phase. It updates its model of the world continuously as new sensory data arrives. When something unexpected happens, the prediction error propagates down, and every layer adjusts.
Hippocampus Episodic
Episodic memory. Stores full sensorimotor experiences as they happen. When the brain encounters a new situation, the hippocampus retrieves the most relevant past experience by cosine similarity. This gives the system a form of experience based reasoning without requiring a training loop. It remembers what worked before.
Basal Ganglia Action selection
The decision maker. Evaluates candidate motor trajectories by computing expected free energy, a quantity that balances two objectives: information gain (exploring unknown states) and goal achievement (reaching desired states). The basal ganglia picks the trajectory that is both safe and informative. This is how the brain explores intelligently without needing a hand crafted reward function.
All five layers are unified by the Free Energy Principle. There is no backpropagation anywhere in the system. No gradient descent. No loss function in the traditional sense. The brain adapts continuously through prediction and correction, the same mechanism that biological nervous systems have used for hundreds of millions of years.
04 — Active Inference explained

Active Inference is a theory of brain function proposed by Karl Friston, a neuroscientist at University College London. The central idea is that all living systems, from single cells to human brains, do one thing: they minimize surprise. In mathematical terms, they minimize variational free energy, which is an upper bound on the surprise of their sensory observations.

Here is how it works in practice. The brain maintains a generative model of the world. It constantly predicts what it should see, hear, and feel at every moment. When sensory data arrives and matches the prediction, nothing happens. When there is a mismatch, the brain has two options: update its model (perception) or act on the world to make the prediction come true (action). This loop, predict then act then correct, runs continuously.

This is fundamentally different from reinforcement learning. RL agents maximize a reward signal. They need someone to define what "good" looks like, and they need millions of trials to figure out how to get there. Active Inference agents do not need a reward function. They are driven by a single imperative: reduce uncertainty. Exploration emerges naturally from the mathematics, because uncertain states have high expected free energy, and the agent is drawn to resolve that uncertainty.

The biological plausibility is what makes this approach powerful for robotics. RL requires massive offline training. Active Inference works in real time. RL is brittle outside its training distribution. Active Inference adapts on the fly because it is always updating its model. RL needs a reward signal. Active Inference only needs a prior belief about what the world should look like.

Every layer of the 20n architecture implements some form of free energy minimization. The spinal cord minimizes proprioceptive prediction error. The cerebellum minimizes forward model error. The cortex minimizes world model error. The basal ganglia minimizes expected free energy over future trajectories. It is one principle, applied at every scale.

05 — Results and benchmarks

The 20n brain has been validated on three standard robot morphologies in MuJoCo, the industry standard physics simulator developed by DeepMind (now part of Google). MuJoCo is the benchmark environment used by virtually every major robotics lab in the world. A score of 1000 represents the maximum achievable performance on a given task.

Robot Joints Max score Mean score Status
Walker2d 6 joints (2D) 1000 952 Near perfect locomotion
Ant 8 joints (3D) 1000 667 Solved
Humanoid 17 joints (3D) 474 163 Hardest benchmark, improving

Walker2d is a planar biped with 6 actuated joints. The brain achieves a mean score of 952 out of 1000, which represents near perfect forward locomotion. The gait is smooth and stable.

Ant is a quadruped with 8 joints operating in full 3D. The brain reaches a maximum score of 1000 and averages 667. It learns to coordinate all four legs within seconds of being dropped into the body.

Humanoid is the most challenging benchmark in the MuJoCo suite. Seventeen joints, full 3D, inherently unstable. The brain currently scores a maximum of 474 and is improving steadily. This is the frontier. Every major lab in the world struggles with Humanoid, and 20n is tackling it with zero training and a fraction of the compute.

The same brain, the same set of parameters, controls all three bodies. There is no retraining between morphologies. No fine tuning. The architecture adapts in real time.

vs. Boston Dynamics

Boston Dynamics uses hand crafted controllers that are manually tuned by teams of engineers for each robot and each behavior. Atlas does impressive backflips, but every motion is the result of years of bespoke engineering. There is no learning, no generalization. Move to a new body and you start from scratch. 20n uses one universal brain. Drop it in any body and it figures things out.

vs. Tesla Optimus

Tesla's approach to Optimus relies on imitation learning. The robot watches massive amounts of human demonstration data and learns to replicate the movements. This requires enormous data collection infrastructure and still produces a system that only does what it has seen. 20n requires zero demonstration data. The brain learns by doing, not by watching.

vs. Figure AI

Figure uses large language models for high level command interpretation, paired with separate low level motor controllers. This creates a two layer system where the LLM understands language but does not understand physics, and the low level controller understands physics but not context. 20n is end to end. One architecture handles perception, world modeling, decision making, and motor control. There is no seam between "thinking" and "doing."

08 — Technical stack

The entire 20n system is built with a deliberately minimal stack. No unnecessary abstractions, no frameworks for the sake of frameworks. Every dependency earns its place.

Python PyTorch MuJoCo NumPy Active Inference CfC / Liquid Neural Networks LTC Networks Free Energy Principle

Python is the primary language. All core logic, the inference loops, the liquid network implementations, the memory system, everything runs in Python.

PyTorch handles the neural network components, specifically the CfC and LTC liquid networks used in the cerebellum and cortex layers. PyTorch is used for its dynamic computation graph, which is essential for continuous time models that change structure based on input.

MuJoCo (Multi Joint dynamics with Contact) is the physics simulator. Developed originally by Emo Todorov and now maintained by DeepMind, MuJoCo is the gold standard for simulating articulated bodies with contact physics. Every benchmark in this project runs inside MuJoCo.

NumPy handles the numerical backbone. The PD controllers in the spinal cord layer, the free energy computations, the cosine similarity in the hippocampus, all of this is NumPy.
