What was Friday

An autonomous AI agent for macOS

Friday was not a chatbot. It was not an assistant that waited for voice commands. It was an autonomous agent that could see the screen, move the mouse, type on the keyboard, and accomplish complex tasks on its own. It operated exactly like a human sitting at a computer, perceiving what was on the display and deciding what to do next.

There were no API integrations, no cloud dependency, no shortcuts or workarounds. Everything happened directly on the device using computer vision and chain of thought reasoning. Friday looked at the screen the same way you would, read the text the same way you would, and clicked the buttons the same way you would. The only difference is that it could do it faster, and it never got tired.

How it worked

See the screen, understand it, act on it

Friday took screenshots of the screen continuously, analyzed them using OCR and vision models, and built a mental model of everything that was visible. It identified buttons, text fields, menus, application windows, and anything else on the display. From that understanding, it decided what actions to take next.

It could click buttons, type text, navigate between applications, scroll through pages, and complete multi step workflows that spanned several different programs. When it encountered a complex task, it used chain of thought reasoning to decompose the problem into smaller, manageable steps, then executed each one in sequence.

One of Friday's most interesting behaviors was tool delegation. It understood that different applications were better suited for different tasks. If it needed to write something, it would open ChatGPT. If it needed to build a presentation, it would open Gamma. For design work, it went to Canva. For research, Perplexity. It chose the best available tool for the job, just like a human would.

1. Screenshot capture the full screen at regular intervals ↓ 2. OCR + Vision extract all text, identify UI elements ↓ 3. State Model build a structured representation of what is on screen ↓ 4. Reasoning chain of thought to decide the next action ↓ 5. Execution mouse click, keyboard input, scroll, or app switch ↓ 6. Verification re-capture, confirm the action succeeded, continue ↺ loop
Capabilities

What Friday could actually do

These were not theoretical features. Nick0 built and demonstrated all of them. Friday could perform complete workflows end to end, without the user needing to touch the mouse or keyboard at any point.

  • Automate presentations from start to finish, including opening the application, writing the content, adding slides, and formatting the layout
  • Draft and send emails by navigating to the mail client, composing the message, reviewing it, and hitting send
  • Research topics across multiple sources, compile findings, and summarize them into a structured document
  • Process PDFs by opening them, extracting key information, and reorganizing the content
  • Navigate complex interfaces with nested menus, dropdown selections, and multi page forms
  • Switch between applications seamlessly, carrying context from one tool to the next
  • Handle errors and unexpected states by recognizing when something went wrong and adapting its approach
Evolution

Two versions, each one sharper than the last

Friday went through two major iterations over the course of the project. Both versions ran entirely on the Mac, with no external servers or cloud processing involved.

V1

The initial proof of concept. Basic screen reading, simple action execution, and straightforward task completion. V1 demonstrated that the core idea worked: an AI could perceive a graphical interface and interact with it through synthetic input. The reasoning was linear and the error handling was minimal, but it was enough to prove the concept was viable.

V2

A significant leap forward. V2 introduced deeper reasoning chains that could plan several steps ahead, more robust error handling that recovered gracefully from unexpected interface states, and improved action planning that chose better strategies for complex tasks. The perception pipeline was also refined, producing more accurate screen understanding.

Why it mattered

The insight that changed everything

Friday proved something fundamental. It showed that an AI brain could perceive and act on a complex digital environment in real time. Not through shortcuts, not through APIs, not through pre programmed scripts. It was literally seeing the interface and interacting with it the same way a human being would.

Most AI tools at the time worked by plugging into existing software through programmatic interfaces. They sent HTTP requests, parsed JSON responses, and manipulated data behind the scenes. Friday did none of that. It worked purely at the visual and motor level, the same level at which humans operate. That distinction matters enormously.

If a brain can control a screen by seeing and acting on it, what happens when the environment is not a screen but a physical robot body? That question became the foundation of 20n.

The perception action loop that powered Friday is, at its core, the same loop that powers biological organisms. You perceive the world through your senses, you build a model of what is happening, you decide what to do, and you act. Friday did exactly this in a digital environment. The natural extension was to ask whether the same architecture could work in the physical world. That question led Nicolas Philippe to leave Friday behind and dedicate everything to robotics.

Why it stopped

The concept was proven. The real challenge was elsewhere.

Controlling a digital screen was phase one. It was the proof of concept, the demonstration that a brain could perceive and act on an environment in real time. But once that was established, the question became obvious: why stop at screens?

The bigger challenge, the one worth dedicating everything to, was physical embodiment. A screen is a controlled, predictable, two dimensional environment. A physical robot body exists in three dimensions, deals with gravity, friction, inertia, and a world that does not pause when you stop to think. That is where the real difficulty lies, and that is where the real impact would come from.

Nicolas Philippe made the decision to go all in on robotics. Friday's architecture principles, the perception action loop, the chain of thought reasoning, the continuous state monitoring, all of it became the conceptual foundation of 20n Research Laboratory. Friday was not abandoned. It graduated.
