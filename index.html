About

I started coding at sixteen in Alsace, France. No one taught me. I picked up Python because I wanted to build trading bots, not because anyone told me it was a good language to learn. That curiosity quickly became obsessive, and within months I was selling websites to local businesses, negotiating contracts, and managing clients. I was sixteen years old, knocking on doors, pitching redesigns to shop owners who had never met a teenager with a business card.

From there, everything accelerated. I launched GlowGum, a beauty chewing gum brand that reached 12,000 TikTok followers in fifteen days. I built NERVE, a full stack Cloud FinOps platform, and Eve, an AI agent that creates and trains AI models from a single conversation. I created Friday, an autonomous agent that could see and control any Mac interface like a human, using pure computer vision and reasoning.

Friday was the turning point. Controlling a screen proved that an AI brain could perceive and act on an environment in real time. The natural question became: what if the environment is not a screen, but a physical body? That question led me to found 20n Research Laboratory in January 2026, where I now build a universal brain for robots grounded in theoretical neuroscience and Active Inference.

My work is now attracting attention from J12 Ventures, Plug and Play, and professors at MIT. I won Innov Hack Paris 2026 among 150 participants. I have no degree, no bootcamp certificate, and no formal education in computer science. Everything I know, I taught myself by building things and reading papers.

Current focus

20n Research Laboratory

A universal artificial brain for humanoid robots. One architecture that can control any body, with zero training, no cloud dependency, and a power consumption of just 20 watts. Grounded in Karl Friston's Active Inference and Liquid Neural Networks developed at MIT.

20W

1kHz

3
Bodies validated

The architecture is biomimetic and hierarchical, modeled directly after the biological brain. Five layers work together at different frequencies: the Spinal Cord handles reflexes at 1kHz, the Cerebellum builds an internal body model at 100Hz, the Cortex learns a world model at 10Hz, the Hippocampus stores episodic memories, and the Basal Ganglia selects actions by evaluating the safest trajectories.

I have benchmarked the system on MuJoCo, the industry standard physics simulator by DeepMind. On Walker2d, the brain reaches a maximum score of 1000, which is near perfect locomotion. On Ant, it also reaches 1000. On Humanoid, the most challenging benchmark with 17 joints in 3D, it currently scores 474 and is improving steadily. The same brain, the same parameters, three radically different robot bodies, zero retraining.
